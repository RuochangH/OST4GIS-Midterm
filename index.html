Divvy Bike-share Station Hourly Departure Projection

Introduction

In 2013, the city of Chicago lauched Divvy bike share system. As a new mode that facilitates short distance travels and transit rides, the system is popular across the town. However, the unbalanced bike flows hamper the operation and expansion of the system. On the one hand, it results in full or empty station frequently, downgrading the reliability of the system. On the other hand, bike reallocation, solution to this problem, is based on real-time activity, which is costy and lagging behind.

While the unbalanced bike flow is inherented, pro-active reallocation has the potential to reduce the cost and reduce loss of customers. Therefore, this project is designed to build a OLS model to project the hourly departure for 147 stations near downtown Loop.

Predictors: Station Area Predictor Exploration

To build the predictive model for hourly departures, we included four type of varibles, namesly spatially-and-temporally lagged hourly depatures, socio-economic and bike network characteristics of station areas, distance to and average ridership of nearest transit stations for each bike station, and time of depatures. The last three types are all theoritically related to bike trip demand, and the first type, including historical depatures at the same station and neighboring station departures, are assumed to be similar to the dependent variable because demands are usually concentrated in certain time period and location.

For purpose of illustration, this page mainly illustrate major station area properties, including employment and population density, median household income, bike lane length, and number of Transit Stations within station area. Station area is defined by 0.5-mile buffer around stations. The origional data comes from ACS census, and Chicago Open Data, and they are summarized and exported in ArcGIS through Spatial Join.
